# Conference-Day Panel Discussion Prompts
## SAEM 2026 AI Consensus Conference — May 21, 2026

Three prompts per WG, synthesized from each working group's curated R2
question set. Each prompt clusters 5–8 related R2 questions so the
breakout discussion drives meaningful coverage rather than scattering
across 20+ questions. Co-leads can use one prompt per breakout round,
or combine 2-3 into a single longer breakout if time allows.

Drafted by chair (R. Andrew Taylor) on 2026-05-13 based on the R2
question sets as of that date.

---

## Panel 1 — Clinical Practice & Operations (WG1: Sangal / Patterson)

**Prompt 1.1 — From validated AI to better clinical outcomes**
How do we move from technically-validated AI outputs to meaningfully
better clinical and operational outcomes in the ED — and what design,
workflow, and governance conditions are required to bridge that gap?
*(Touches Q1, Q3, Q5, Q6, Q13, Q14, Q131, Q135, Q139, Q143.)*

**Prompt 1.2 — Managing the AI portfolio**
As EDs deploy multiple AI systems (imaging, triage, sepsis, LLMs,
scribes), how should we approach validation, lifecycle monitoring,
build-vs-buy decisions, and clinician adoption when the unit of
intervention is no longer a single tool but a portfolio?
*(Touches Q8, Q11, Q134, Q136, Q144, Q145, Q175.)*

**Prompt 1.3 — Reaching everyone in the ED**
What does equitable AI implementation look like for historically
understudied populations (behavioral health, pediatrics, rural,
non-English-speaking), and where are the biggest infrastructure or
organizational gaps preventing this from happening at scale?
*(Touches Q10, Q132, Q141.)*

## Panel 2 — Infrastructure & Data Ecosystems (WG2: Riley / Abbott)

**Prompt 2.1 — A trustworthy data foundation for ED AI**
What does the minimum viable data foundation look like for an ED to
safely deploy AI — covering data quality, SDOH capture,
de-identification, governance, and ongoing drift monitoring — and where
are the biggest gaps today?
*(Touches Q15, Q17, Q35, Q149, Q156, Q160, Q162.)*

**Prompt 2.2 — Bias from the data layer up**
How can the EM community detect, measure, and mitigate algorithmic
bias that originates in training data, especially for under-represented
populations, and what infrastructure investments (benchmarks,
validation methods, governance) most reliably reduce that bias?
*(Touches Q21, Q22, Q24, Q26, Q36, Q161, Q164.)*

**Prompt 2.3 — Sharing without losing control**
What are the right structures for multi-institutional collaboration on
ED AI (federated learning, data-sharing agreements, dynamic consent,
synthetic data, cross-institutional benchmarks) given the constraints
of emergency care and competing institutional interests?
*(Touches Q19, Q23, Q28, Q29, Q33, Q34, Q37, Q154, Q155, Q157, Q168.)*

## Panel 3 — Education, Training & Competency (WG3: Rose / Preiksaitis)

**Prompt 3.1 — What does AI competency mean for EM, concretely?**
How should AI competencies be defined and tiered across the EM
training continuum (medical student → attending → educator), and how
do we anchor them in existing frameworks (ACGME Milestones, ABEM MCP,
EM Foundations 3) without bolting on more checklists?
*(Touches Q40, Q41, Q42, Q43, Q44, Q46, Q47, Q55, Q58.)*

**Prompt 3.2 — The reasoning question**
Does sustained AI use erode diagnostic reasoning, autonomy, or skill
retention — and if so, what training and workflow interventions
preserve clinical judgment while still capturing AI's benefits? How
do we teach calibrated trust and hallucination detection?
*(Touches Q48, Q50, Q51, Q52, Q56, Q57, Q177.)*

**Prompt 3.3 — The patient on the other side**
As patients arrive with chatbot differentials, AI-summarized charts,
and consumer wearable interpretations, what competencies do EM
physicians need to manage these encounters, and how should we be
communicating with patients about AI-generated recommendations?
*(Touches Q49, Q53, Q176.)*

## Panel 4 — Human-AI Interaction & Perception of Self (WG4: Yiadom / Rehman / Declan)

**Prompt 4.1 — What AI does to the clinician**
How does AI integration affect physician diagnostic reasoning,
autonomy, professional identity, burnout, and well-being — and where
is the line between augmentation that supports clinicians vs. erosion
of the skills and judgment that define the specialty?
*(Touches Q60, Q61, Q62, Q66, Q69, Q71, Q75, Q77, Q90.)*

**Prompt 4.2 — The patient experience**
What does informed consent, trust, and the clinician-patient
relationship look like when AI sits between physician and patient —
and how do we handle the new dynamic of patients arriving with their
own AI-generated information?
*(Touches Q74, Q78, Q79, Q81, Q82, Q84, Q86.)*

**Prompt 4.3 — When the system disagrees**
How should clinicians and ED systems handle the moments where AI
recommendations conflict with clinical intuition, where responsibility
shifts after an AI-assisted decision causes harm, and where automation
bias quietly distorts care?
*(Touches Q64, Q85, Q88, Q171, Q172, Q173, Q174.)*

---

## Panel 5 — Ethical, Legal & Societal Implications (WG5: Hall / Declan / Sumathipala)

**Prompt 5.1 — Liability and the standard of care**
How should the reasonable-physician standard apply when AI is in the
loop, and how should liability be distributed across developers,
health systems, and clinicians when an AI-assisted decision causes
harm?
*(Touches Q91, Q96, Q99, Q100, Q110.)*

**Prompt 5.2 — Equity at scale**
What legal frameworks, institutional accountability mechanisms, and
design practices are needed to ensure AI doesn't worsen access for
marginalized populations, doesn't enable insurance-driven care
refusals, and accounts for downstream environmental and socioeconomic
costs?
*(Touches Q103, Q104, Q121, Q125, Q128, Q169.)*

**Prompt 5.3 — Governance vs. care delivery**
As AI is deployed across pre-ED, ED, and post-ED care, and as it
expands APP scope and changes safe-staffing dynamics, what
institutional governance, oversight, and patient-rights mechanisms
keep the system tethered to patient safety and the ethical foundations
of EM?
*(Touches Q107, Q109, Q112, Q117, Q127, Q170.)*
