# Working Group 4 — Human-AI Interaction

**SAEM 2026 AI Consensus Conference · 2-page summary**
Co-leads: Maame Yiadom · Tehreem Rehman
Pillar: Self

---

## Background

AI is changing the cognitive and relational work of emergency physicians. Decision-support systems sit between clinicians and the data they reason from; ambient scribes mediate the patient encounter; patients arrive with their own AI-generated differentials. WG4 asks how AI affects clinician reasoning, autonomy, professional identity, and wellbeing, and how AI integration affects the clinician-patient relationship that emergency medicine depends on. The agenda spans first-order cognitive effects (reasoning, reliance, conditions that enhance or impair judgment), workforce effects (burnout, autonomy, satisfaction), trust and explainability (what clinicians and patients need to safely act on AI outputs), and the boundary between human oversight and AI autonomy. Without a research base on these human-factors questions, AI deployment risks degrading the cognitive and relational core of emergency medicine even as it improves measurable workflow metrics.

## Final research questions

**1. AI vs. clinical intuition.** What is the experience of emergency physicians when AI recommendations conflict with their clinical intuition, and how do they resolve that conflict in real time?
*Pain point:* AI-clinician disagreement happens routinely in deployed CDS but has not been studied. *Conceptual expansion:* Real-time observation and structured debrief to characterize the heuristics clinicians use to resolve conflict. *Anticipated impact:* Surfaces the cognitive work of override decisions and informs CDS design that supports judgment.

**2. AI and the clinician-patient relationship.** What are the effects of AI on the quality and nature of the clinician-patient relationship in emergency care (accuracy, timeliness, continuity, trust)?
*Pain point:* AI mediates encounters through scribes, CDS, and patient-facing tools, but the net effect on the bond is unknown. *Conceptual expansion:* Measure relationship-quality outcomes across deployment models that vary in AI visibility. *Anticipated impact:* Protects the patient relationship at the center of EM from being eroded by efficiency-only tools.

**3. Risks of clinician reliance on AI.** What are the risks of physician reliance on AI in emergency medicine, and what mitigations preserve clinician skill?
*Pain point:* Over-reliance is a well-documented failure mode in other safety-critical industries; EM lacks specific evidence. *Conceptual expansion:* Identify reliance patterns, measurable skill degradation, and the interventions that keep clinicians calibrated. *Anticipated impact:* Names the boundary between productive augmentation and harmful reliance.

**4. AI effects on diagnostic reasoning.** How does AI-augmented clinical decision making affect emergency physicians' diagnostic reasoning processes?
*Pain point:* AI changes what clinicians notice and rule out, but the cognitive mechanism is poorly characterized. *Conceptual expansion:* Protocol-analysis and observational studies of how AI prompts reshape reasoning step by step. *Anticipated impact:* Reveals the pathway from AI input to decision; informs CDS that supports rather than short-circuits reasoning.

**5. Useful vs. distracting explainability.** What forms of AI explainability are clinically useful vs. distracting in ED workflows?
*Pain point:* Explainability is treated as universally good, but ED workflows have no time for explanation that doesn't change action. *Conceptual expansion:* Compare explanation modalities (feature attribution, prototypes, counterfactuals) for ED-appropriate utility and cognitive load. *Anticipated impact:* Drives explainability design toward forms that improve clinical decisions.

**6. CDS integration without cognitive overload.** How can AI-augmented decision support be implemented in existing ED workflows without disrupting care or increasing cognitive burden?
*Pain point:* AI tools layer alerts and prompts onto saturated workflows, worsening alert fatigue. *Conceptual expansion:* Study integration strategies that minimize interruption and quantify cognitive load. *Anticipated impact:* Aligns AI deployment with the cognitive limits of ED clinicians.

**7. Burnout, moral injury, and AI integration.** How does AI integration affect ED professional satisfaction and stress, including burnout and moral injury?
*Pain point:* AI is positioned as a burnout intervention but deployment may add cognitive load and dilute agency. *Conceptual expansion:* Track satisfaction, burnout, and moral injury longitudinally across deployment patterns. *Anticipated impact:* Determines whether AI is an answer to or contributor to EM workforce attrition.

**8. Conditions enhancing or impairing decisions.** Under what conditions does AI enhance or impair ED clinical decision making?
*Pain point:* AI effects depend on alertness, case mix, time pressure, and team dynamics — the conditions are not mapped. *Conceptual expansion:* Identify case, team, and environmental conditions that change outcome. *Anticipated impact:* Lets deployment be tuned to conditions where AI helps and dampened where it doesn't.

**9. Mitigating bias and inequity in CDS implementation.** Which ED AI implementation strategies best mitigate unintended bias and inequities across patient subgroups?
*Pain point:* Even well-designed AI produces biased outcomes when deployment context interacts with patient demographics. *Conceptual expansion:* Compare deployment strategies (silent mode, threshold tuning, subgroup monitoring) in real use. *Anticipated impact:* Operationalizes equity in deployment, not just training.

**10. Clinician autonomy under AI integration.** How does AI integration impact emergency physicians' autonomy?
*Pain point:* AI prompts shift decisional authority from clinicians to algorithms and the institutions that deploy them. *Conceptual expansion:* Measure perceived and structural autonomy across integration models and deployment intensities. *Anticipated impact:* Names the conditions under which AI augments vs. erodes the autonomy EM depends on.

**11. Patient trust in AI involvement.** What factors shape patient trust in AI involvement in their clinical care (disclosure, explanation, consent)?
*Pain point:* Patients may not know AI is involved; even when they do, trust determinants are unmapped. *Conceptual expansion:* Identify disclosure, explanation, and consent practices that calibrate trust across demographics. *Anticipated impact:* Patient-facing communication standards for AI deployment that respects patient agency.

**12. Adaptive AI that learns from clinician feedback.** How should adaptive AI systems that learn from clinician feedback be designed and governed to ensure they improve rather than degrade over time?
*Pain point:* Continuously learning AI can drift in response to biased feedback, magnifying existing patterns. *Conceptual expansion:* Specify feedback structures, monitoring metrics, and human-in-the-loop governance. *Anticipated impact:* Framework for adopting adaptive AI without compounding bias.

## Thematic summary

Three threads run through the WG4 agenda. First, **cognition and reasoning under AI augmentation** — Q1, Q3, Q4, and Q8 address how AI changes what clinicians notice, hypothesize, and decide. Second, **trust, oversight, and adaptation** — Q5, Q6, and Q12 form the thread of how explainability, workflow integration, and adaptive feedback shape calibrated trust over time. Third, **workforce identity and the clinician-patient bond** — Q2, Q7, Q10, and Q11 capture AI's effects on what physicians experience (autonomy, burnout) and what patients perceive (relationship quality, trust).

## Conclusion and call to action

WG4 calls for prospective human-factors studies — in-situ observation, simulation, and longitudinal cognitive measurement — funded by AHRQ patient-safety mechanisms and SAEM human-factors grants, with the goal of evidence-based AI deployment standards that protect cognition, autonomy, and the clinician-patient relationship.
