# Working Group 3 — Education & Training

**SAEM 2026 AI Consensus Conference · 2-page summary**
Co-leads: Christian Rose · Carl Preiksaitis
Pillar: Training

---

## Background

AI tools have moved from research artifact to point-of-care reality in emergency medicine, with ambient documentation, sepsis surveillance, and imaging triage now deployed at thousands of EDs. Yet the training, assessment, and faculty-development infrastructure that prepares EM physicians to work with AI lags far behind the technology. ACGME EM Milestones 2.0, the ABEM Model, and EM Foundations 3 do not yet anchor AI-specific competencies; residents calibrate trust ad hoc; and faculty have no published model for teaching critical appraisal of AI outputs. The cognitive consequences of sustained AI use — on diagnostic reasoning, autonomy, and skill retention — remain unstudied even as deployment accelerates. Patients now arrive with their own AI-generated differentials, expanding what physicians must be trained to handle. WG3 asks what trainees and attendings need to know, how those competencies are taught and assessed, and how to preserve reasoning skills in an AI-augmented practice environment.

## Final research questions

**1. Sustained AI use → diagnostic reasoning, autonomy, and skill.** Does sustained AI use affect EM physician diagnostic reasoning, autonomy, or skill retention over time?
*Pain point:* AI decision-support is embedded in routine workflows, yet the longitudinal cognitive consequences are unstudied. *Conceptual expansion:* Compare reasoning, autonomy, and skill retention between high- and low-exposure physicians across training and practice. *Anticipated impact:* Determines whether AI augments or degrades EM's core reasoning skills.

**2. Anchoring competencies in ACGME / ABEM / EM Foundations.** How should EM AI competencies be anchored in ACGME Milestones 2.0, ABEM Model, and EM Foundations 3?
*Pain point:* No EM accreditation framework currently names AI-specific competencies. *Conceptual expansion:* Map proposed competencies onto existing milestones, ABEM Model, and EM Foundations 3. *Anticipated impact:* Embeds AI training in the infrastructure programs are already accountable to.

**3. LLM delegation vs. physician-led.** Which cognitive tasks are appropriate for LLM delegation vs. physician-led decision-making?
*Pain point:* LLMs are used for documentation, reasoning, and patient communication without a shared delegation framework. *Conceptual expansion:* Categorize ED cognitive work by complexity, risk, and reversibility. *Anticipated impact:* Defensible framework for appropriate LLM use.

**4. Hallucination and error detection across training stages.** How reliably do EM physicians at different training stages detect LLM hallucinations and errors?
*Pain point:* Detection is a critical safety skill but baseline ability varies widely and is not formally assessed. *Conceptual expansion:* Measure detection reliability across PGY level, experience, and AI training. *Anticipated impact:* Floor of LLM-safety competency and high-yield remediation stages.

**5. Patient communication about AI recommendations.** How should EM physicians communicate with patients about AI-generated recommendations, including patient-initiated AI?
*Pain point:* Physicians lack scripts and vocabulary for AI involvement, including patients who arrive with their own AI differentials. *Conceptual expansion:* Develop and validate communication frameworks for disclosure, trust calibration, and response to patient-initiated AI. *Anticipated impact:* Preserves trust as AI becomes a routine participant in care.

**6. Competency → patient-safety outcomes.** Does EM physician AI competency predict patient-level safety outcomes?
*Pain point:* AI competency is treated as intrinsically valuable, but downstream effects on patient safety have not been demonstrated. *Conceptual expansion:* Link validated competency measures to patient-level outcomes across multi-program cohorts. *Anticipated impact:* Evidence base justifying competency-driven curriculum reform.

**7. Faculty development for AI.** What interventions prepare EM attendings to teach, assess, and role-model AI use?
*Pain point:* No published EM-specific faculty development for AI exists. *Conceptual expansion:* Design and evaluate interventions covering technical literacy, assessment, and role-modeling. *Anticipated impact:* Removes the most common bottleneck to embedding AI competencies in residency.

**8. Resident trust calibration over training.** How do EM residents calibrate trust in AI outputs over training, and what environment features shape calibration?
*Pain point:* Residents calibrate trust through informal experience that varies across programs. *Conceptual expansion:* Track calibration longitudinally and identify learning-environment features that shape it. *Anticipated impact:* Identifies structured experiences and feedback that produce calibrated trust by graduation.

**9. Competencies for patient-initiated AI.** What competencies enable EM physicians to manage patient-initiated AI use (chatbot differentials, AI-summarized chart access, wearables)?
*Pain point:* Patient-initiated AI is common but no competency framework names the skills needed to manage it. *Conceptual expansion:* Define knowledge, communication, and reasoning skills required for real-time engagement. *Anticipated impact:* Equips physicians for encounters where patients arrive pre-equipped with AI-generated information.

**10. Pre-emptive vs. post-hoc integration models.** How do training needs differ for pre-emptive vs. post-hoc AI integration models?
*Pain point:* Pre-emptive (AI before clinician review) and post-hoc (AI checks clinician decisions) demand fundamentally different cognitive workflows. *Conceptual expansion:* Compare cognitive workload, error patterns, and training needs between paradigms. *Anticipated impact:* Tailors training to the specific cognitive demands of each integration model.

## Thematic summary

Three threads run through the WG3 agenda. First, **anchoring competencies in existing frameworks** — Q2 (ACGME/ABEM/EM Foundations) and Q7 (faculty development) tie AI training to the accreditation and program infrastructure EM already depends on, rather than building a parallel curriculum. Second, **cognitive effects of sustained AI use** — Q1, Q4, Q6, and Q8 form a longitudinal thread tracking diagnostic reasoning, hallucination detection, safety outcomes, and trust calibration as physicians work with AI over time. Third, **patient-facing AI and the changing encounter** — Q5, Q9, and Q10 recognize that AI is no longer behind the curtain: patients bring AI to the encounter, integration paradigms differ, and physician communication has to adapt.

## Conclusion and call to action

WG3 will operationalize these competencies through ACGME/ABEM curriculum proposals, validated workplace-based assessment tools, and multi-program faculty-development trials — funded primarily via training-grant pathways (R25, K12) and partnerships with CORD, SAEM RAMS, and the ABEM education committee.
